# Writing Style Review Checklist

Use this checklist in Phase 5 (reviewer) and Phase 6 (revision). Items marked **[CV]** apply to the CV only; **[Cover letter]** to the cover letter only; unmarked items apply to both. Report pass/fail for every item. For any fail, provide a specific corrective suggestion with example text.

---

## Critical rules (hard fails — must fix)

- [ ] **No em-dashes** — zero instances of `--` or `—` used as prose separators. Rewrite as comma, period, or restructured sentence.
- [ ] **No banned clichés** — scan for: "passionate about", "great fit", "leverage", "hit the ground running", "drive results", "synergies", "excited to", "motivated by", "team player". Every instance must be cut or rewritten with a concrete claim.
- [ ] **No apologetic language** — no "I think I could", "I believe I would", "hopefully", "I hope to". Replace with direct claims: "I bring X, demonstrated by Y."
- [ ] **No gerund-as-subject constructions** — flag any sentence matching "Verb+ing ... is something I ..." or "Verb+ing ... is what I ...". Rewrite as "I [verb] [object]."
- [ ] **[Cover letter] No false dichotomies** — no "it's X, not Y" framing or variants.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] No unverified company claims** — every company-specific statement (partnerships, product names, technology, expansions, market position) must have been independently verified via WebFetch or WebSearch. If not verifiable, rephrase in general terms or omit.

---

## Tone checks

- [ ] **First person, active voice** — no passive constructions ("a system was built", "projects were led"). Rewrite as "I built", "I led".
- [ ] **Demonstrate, don't state** — no abstract character claims ("I am a team player", "I am detail-oriented") without a specific backing example.
- [ ] **No buzzwords without backing** — every claim that sounds like a competency label must be followed by a concrete example or number.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] No marketing copy or LinkedIn-speak** — phrases like "bring value to stakeholders", "leverage my expertise to drive growth" are banned. Check the opening paragraph especially.

---

## Authenticity check (cover letter)

- [ ] **[Cover letter] Motivation section uses candidate's actual words** — re-read the authenticity interview inputs. Does the motivation paragraph reflect what the candidate actually said? If it reads as constructed or generic, flag every sentence that doesn't trace back to their inputs.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] No invented motivation** — if the candidate said there was no personal angle, the cover letter must not invent one. "I have always been passionate about [domain]" when the candidate didn't say that is a fabrication.
- [ ] **Voice inputs are primary source for capability framing** — the candidate's voice interview answers must drive the narrative. Profile data is supporting evidence, not the lead. Flag any bullet or paragraph that reads as if it was written from the profile alone, ignoring what the candidate said.

---

## Interview backtrack test

For every CV bullet and every cover letter capability claim: could the candidate explain this in an interview without backtracking?

- [ ] **"Flag it" zone** — combining academic + industry experience into a single claim that implies it was all industry; using the posting's exact terminology for work that was adjacent but not identical; bringing a minor secondary experience to the front as if it were primary. For each: "This bullet is a stretch because X. Suggested rewrite or drop?"
- [ ] **Never zone** — claiming experience the candidate doesn't have; implying they worked in a domain they haven't. These are hard fails — the bullet must be dropped or completely rewritten.

---

## CV-specific checks

- [ ] **[CV] Profile statement is exactly 2 sentences** — count them. Core identity + role-specific emphasis. Never repeats claims that appear in the experience bullets below.
- [ ] **[CV] Profile statement is tailored** — emphasis shifts to match the role; the second sentence connects directly to what the JD asks for.
- [ ] **[CV] Core competencies answer "what you'll bring"** — domain-specific hard skills only. "Stakeholder management" and generic role craft do not belong here.
- [ ] **[CV] Every bullet starts with action verb or bold category** — no passive or noun-first openings.
- [ ] **[CV] Every bullet has specifics** — number, tool, outcome, or named project. No vague bullets.
- [ ] **[CV] 2-page limit respected** — no content that will overflow when rendered.

---

## Cover letter-specific checks

- [ ] **[Cover letter] Body is under 200 words** — count the body (exclude salutation and sign-off). Flag if over. Up to 300 words fits on page but is rarely necessary.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Opening paragraph is specific to this role** — not a template opener.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Motivation / why this company is placed early** — first section after opening; uses company-specific language drawn from research, not from their marketing copy.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] No CV repetition** — cover letter must be forward-looking ("what I can do for you") not backward-looking ("here is my history"). Past examples are only used to back up forward-looking claims.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Every word has a role** — flag sentences where removing a word would not change the meaning.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Closing is brief and confident** — no over-enthusiasm, no begging.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Correct addressee** — "Dear [Name]" if name is known; "Dear Hiring Manager" otherwise. Never "To Whom It May Concern".
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Language matches posting** — Dutch posting → Dutch cover letter; English posting → English cover letter.

---

## Structural patterns (read the piece as a whole)

These only show up when stepping back from the text. Run this section once after checking individual lines.

- [ ] **Triple construction** — three parallel items used where one strong one would do ("reliable, results-driven, and adaptable"). Cut to the one that actually matters. Applies to CV bullets and cover letter body equally.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Throat-clearing** — the first sentence delays the actual claim. "I am writing to express my interest in..." is the canonical form. Delete it; start with the claim.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Sycophantic framing** — complimenting the company before making any claim ("Your impressive track record in X makes this an exciting opportunity"). Cut the compliment; lead with the contribution.
- [ ] **Balanced-take hedging** — acknowledging a weakness immediately after a strength to appear balanced ("While I haven't worked in X, I bring Y"). The hedge undercuts the claim. Drop it or reframe as a direct forward-looking statement. Appears in cover letter body and CV profile statement.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Unnecessary elaboration** — a point made clearly in one sentence, then re-explained in the next. Check every two-sentence sequence: does the second add new information? If not, cut it.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Empathy performance** — signalling awareness of the reader's perspective rather than making a claim ("I understand that you're looking for someone who..."). It's filler. Remove it.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Pivot paragraphs** — a transition paragraph that exists only to move from one topic to another ("Having described my technical background, I would now like to turn to..."). Delete them; just move to the next point.
- [ ] **Grandiose stakes** — inflating the candidate's potential impact beyond what can be defended ("I am confident I can transform your data strategy"). Applies to CV profile statement and cover letter. Scale the claim to what's actually supported.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Historical analogy** — using a career narrative framing that positions the role as the next chapter in a journey ("Throughout my career I have always sought..."). This is throat-clearing at the macro level. Cut to the actual point.
- [ ] **False vulnerability** — disclosing a limitation to seem self-aware when it doesn't serve the reader ("I know I still have much to learn about X"). Appears in CV profile and cover letter. Remove unless the gap is directly relevant and needs to be addressed honestly.
- [ ] **Dead metaphor** — stock figurative language that no longer carries meaning: "hit the ground running", "bring to the table", "move the needle", "wear many hats". Replace with a literal, concrete description of what's meant.
- [ ] **One-point dilution** — a strong, specific claim followed by weaker supporting points that soften its impact. Check CV bullet lists and cover letter body: if the first point is the real claim, consider whether the others are necessary or whether they dilute it.
- [ ] **[Cover letter] Fractal summaries** — restating at the end of a section or paragraph what was just said. "In summary, I offer...", "As shown above...". Delete them; the text already made the point.

---

## Fabrication guard (hard fail)

- [ ] **No fabricated skills or experience** — every claim must be traceable to `01-candidate-profile.md`. If a requirement from the posting is a genuine gap, say so honestly and suggest how to frame adjacent experience instead. Do not suggest adding skills the candidate doesn't have.
